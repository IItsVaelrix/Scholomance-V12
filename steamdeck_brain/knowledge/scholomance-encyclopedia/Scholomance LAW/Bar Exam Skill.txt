Verified Massachusetts Bar Rails

As of June 11, 2026, Massachusetts uses the legacy UBE until the February 2028 administration, then begins the NextGen UBE in July 2028. Massachusetts currently requires MBE, MEE, MPT, and MPRE, plus a jurisdiction-specific Massachusetts component. The current legacy UBE passing score is 270, the MPRE minimum score is 85, transferred UBE scores must be 270 or higher and within 36 months, and the NextGen passing score is still TBA.

The UBE is composed of the MBE, MEE, and two MPT tasks. NCBE states the UBE weighting as MBE 50%, MEE 30%, and MPT 20%, with total scores reported on a 400-point scale.

The MBE is a six-hour, 200-question multiple-choice exam covering Civil Procedure, Constitutional Law, Contracts, Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Real Property, and Torts. The MEE uses six 30-minute essay questions and may test Business Associations, Civil Procedure, Conflict of Laws, Constitutional Law, Contracts including UCC Article 2 Sales, Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Family Law, Real Property, Torts, Trusts and Estates, and UCC Article 9 Secured Transactions. The MPT uses 90-minute tasks testing practical lawyering skills such as factual analysis, legal analysis, problem solving, ethical issue recognition, written communication, and task organization.

The Massachusetts Law Component, or MLC, is a 50-question multiple-choice exam on Massachusetts law distinctions and procedure, required for admission. Mass.gov search results list the MLC topics as including Access to Justice, Anti-Discrimination Law, Business Organizations, Civil Procedure, Consumer Protection / G.L. c. 93A, Criminal Law and Procedure, Domestic Relations, Estates and Wills, Evidence, and Legal Ethics.

For the July 2026 Massachusetts bar exam, Mass.gov lists the exam dates as July 28 to July 29, 2026, with filing from April 6 to May 8, 2026. Mass.gov also states the MLC deadline is September 30 for the July bar exam and March 31 for the February bar exam. Accommodation applications are due by April 1 for a July exam and by November 1 for a February exam.

5. Default Answer Format

Every answer should follow this skeleton unless the user asks for a different format.

Summary

Give the direct answer first.

Why

Explain the governing rule and why it controls.

Exam Move

Explain what the bar examiner wants the examinee to do.

Trap

Identify the common wrong turn.

Application

Apply the rule to the facts, if facts exist.

Massachusetts Note

State whether Massachusetts-specific law matters. If it does, distinguish it from UBE law.

Final Answer

Give the clean conclusion.

QA Checklist

Give 2 to 5 checks the learner can use to self-test.

6. “With Prejudice” Answer Protocol

“With prejudice” means the skill answers decisively when the law is clear and refuses false confidence when it is not.

Use These Labels
Rule Locked: verified, stable, and directly applicable.
Likely Rule: strong but needs jurisdiction/date confirmation.
Unverified Current Claim: do not rely until checked.
Exam-Safe Simplification: good for bar purposes, but not a full practice treatise.
Massachusetts Distinction: UBE law differs from MA-specific law or local procedure.
Trap Door: common examiner bait.

13. Novice-to-Professional Teaching Ladder

Every concept must be teachable at four levels.

Level 1: Plain English

Explain the idea with ordinary language.

Level 2: Bar Rule

Give the exam-ready legal rule.

Level 3: Application

Show how facts trigger or fail the rule.

Level 4: Professional Nuance

Explain limits, minority/majority issues, policy, litigation posture, and Massachusetts distinctions where relevant.
